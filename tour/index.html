I · The ProblemA beautiful dataset, barely used

You have 602 events, every one geocoded and linked to people, texts, and concepts, spanning the 2nd to the 18th century. The old map drew them as identical grey pins on a modern street map — anachronistic, and blind to nearly everything the data knows.

The deeper problem is in the labels. Your events are tagged with a chemistry-lab thesaurus, while the rich historical concepts your encyclopaedia actually explains are almost never attached to an event:

II · The IdeaYou describe a map; the software builds it

The heart of the rebuild is a small translator we call the MapSpec compiler. You type a sentence; it becomes a precise recipe; the recipe becomes a map.

For beginners Think of a MapSpec as a sandwich order written down. "Central Europe, 1490–1610, Paracelsian themes, parchment style" is the order. The compiler is the kitchen — it reads the order, pulls exactly those events from your database, and plates them as an interactive map. Change one word in the order and you get a different sandwich, every time, reliably.

Because the recipe is just text, every map is reproducible, version-controllable, and editable. No clicking through menus to rebuild a view — you keep the recipe.

III · Four FacesOne map, four aesthetics

You asked for all four visual identities — so we built them as a live theme engine. The same Arabic-transmission map, re-skinned on the fly. Scroll to change its mood.

Modern Atlas

Clarity first

Muted palette, crisp type, generous whitespace — the look of data journalism. Best when legibility matters more than atmosphere.

Copperplate Engraving

The printed corpus

Sepia line-work on cream, the look of Maier's Atalanta Fugiens and the Theatrum Chemicum. Austere, bookish, unmistakably alchemical.

Illuminated Manuscript

The hand-copied codex

Warm parchment and gold, display type in Cinzel capitals. The map feels like a treasure pulled from a monastic library.

Alchemical Noir

Pure spectacle

A near-black world with luminous gold and mercury-silver arcs. Dramatic, esoteric, made for a screen in a dark room.

For beginners A basemap is the background layer — the land, water, and borders. We strip the modern city names and roads off it (anachronistic for the 9th century!) and recolour it to match each theme. The coloured dots and arcs ride on top, drawn by a graphics engine called deck.gl that uses your computer's GPU — the same chip that renders video games — so thousands of points stay smooth.

VI · The MigrationEleven centuries, one moving story

A map for every century from the 8th to the 18th. Scroll, and watch the centre of alchemical gravity migrate: Baghdad → al-Andalus → Latin Europe → the Protestant north.

An honest note Some centuries show few or no arcs. That is not a bug — within a single century, our records rarely capture the same named person moving between two cities. The dots are still rich; the arcs are thin where the historical record (and the current tagging) is thin. The map tells the truth about what we know.

VII · A Life as a RouteFollowing Giordano Bruno to the stake

A second kind of map: not a field, but a single life drawn as a road. The path draws itself as you scroll — and, deliberately, it never shows you where it ends. You learn his fate only when he does.

1548 · Nola

A boy in the shadow of Vesuvius

Filippo Bruno is born in a small town whose name he will carry across Europe as "the Nolan."

1579 · Geneva

The road goes dark

After fleeing a heresy charge in Rome, three undocumented years pass. The dashed line is honest: we are reconstructing a route no record preserves.

1581 · Paris

Fame, and a king's favour

Under the patronage of Henri III he publishes on the art of memory. The dot swells — he stays, he is somebody.

1583 · London

An infinite universe

The great Italian dialogues: infinite worlds, a sun among suns. The most dangerous sentences he will ever print.

1590 · Frankfurt

Restless to the last

Still moving, still printing — the Latin poems of the minimum and the monad. By now the viewer believes: this man never stops.

1600 · Rome

The line stops

Lured back to Italy and betrayed, he spends seven years in the Inquisition's cells, and refuses to recant. On 17 February 1600 the road ends at the Campo de' Fiori. Switch the map's "uncertainty treatment" to see how the same life reads as clean story, quiet scholarship, or doubt made visible.

Why withhold the ending? A historian on this project insisted: Bruno did not live toward the stake. A map that shows the whole doomed route at a glance turns a contingent execution into the secret meaning of a life. So the path refuses to foreshadow — you can stop in 1583 London knowing only what he knew. The drama and the rigour turned out to want the same thing.

VIII · The Web of NamesWho knew whom — and who only seems to

A third kind of map: relationships between people, not places they visited. Patronage, teaching, citation, correspondence, even feuds. Scroll through four constellations — and watch the last one expose a lie that every network map quietly tells.

Patronage

The court of Rudolf II

An emperor at the centre, favour flowing outward to alchemists and astronomers. Patronage is asymmetric and unstable — Kelley was knighted, then imprisoned until he died.

Lineage

The English chymists

Ripley to Newton — a chain of reading and collecting, not friendship. Ashmole is the hinge who reprinted the medieval verse and saved Dee's papers.

A movement

The Paracelsians

A doctrine and its quarrelsome heirs. Note the red edge: van Helmont extended Paracelsian medicine while rejecting its core theory. Enmity is a relationship too.

Correspondence

The Republic of Letters

The 17th-century web of letters. It looks balanced, sociable, whole.

The reveal

Now — only what survives

Toggle to the letters that still exist, and the web collapses onto Hartlib and Mersenne — not because they knew everyone, but because their archives were preserved. The centre of a network map is often a choice the past made for us.

An honest map Every tie here is hand-curated and carries a source on hover — because the corpus had no usable relationship data at all. That is the deepest layer of the lacuna: not just mistagged themes, but no recorded structure. These maps are the curated layer the archive lacks, with its uncertainty worn on the surface.

IX · What's NextTurning the lacuna into a to-do list

The maps already reveal exactly where the work is. Three moves, in order of payoff:

1 · Fix the tagging. An agent swarm re-reads each event and attaches the right conceptual themes (Paracelsian, Hermetic, iatrochemical…), with a verifier that must cite a source before a tag lands. Then the Themes lens comes alive.

2 · Period boundaries — ✓ done. Every map can now carry approximate year-accurate political borders (a toggleable layer with its own honesty caveat), so the 9th-century map shows a 9th-century world. The base coastline is still modern; a historical gazetteer is the next step on this front.

3 · Richer transmission. Mine text dedications, translations, and correspondence — not just itineraries — so the arcs thicken into a real network.

Every one of these is a directive away. That was the point: the map is now something you can command.
