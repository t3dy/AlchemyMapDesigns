Women in the Art

The female practitioners the record kept — and how few it kept

Open full screen ↗View the recipe (JSON) ↗8 events · 2 places · 0 arcs · borders c. 500

Maria the Jewess, whose bain-marie still carries her name; Theosebeia, Zosimos's addressee and collaborator; Hildegard's convent natural philosophy; Anna Zieglerin staking her life on the lion's blood at a ducal court. A handful of dots spanning fourteen centuries — and that is precisely the exhibit: the tradition had women throughout, and the record kept almost none of them.

Design — Illuminated Manuscript.

Tools: MapLibre GL canvas · deck.gl: ScatterplotLayer (points) · ArcLayer (transmission) · time scrubber · TextLayer (labels) · GeoJsonLayer (coastlines · lakes · rivers) · GeoJsonLayer (borders c. 500)

Directive: map the women of alchemy across the whole tradition

Persons = the five women with events in the corpus (8 events total, 3rd–16th c.). The sparsity is the subject: it measures the gendered survival of evidence, not the historical participation of women. Kleopatra the Alchemist is in the corpus vocabulary but carries no dated, geocoded event, so she cannot appear — which proves the point. Borders shown are c. 500 and fit only the late-antique stops; treat them as backdrop, not claim.

Ideas this map is trying

The MapSpec compiler
The core idea of the whole project: a small JSON recipe sits between fuzzy natural language and deterministic rendering. You (or a Claude Code skill) describe a map; the description becomes a versionable contract; a Python generator compiles it to a finished artifact. Change one word in the recipe and rebuild — the variation is reproducible, diffable, and honest about what was asked for. Every data map in this gallery has its recipe linked at the top of its page.

Read more: Toolkit §3 — the MapSpec contract · Manual §3 — directing a map in plain language

Period-accurate borders
Early-modern events used to float over 21st-century borders. Now a spec can say historical_boundaries_year: 1560 and the compiler embeds approximate period polities from a locally cached, slimmed historical-basemaps dataset (snapped to the nearest cached year). It ships as a toggleable Borders layer — hover a line for the polity's name — and the map automatically gains a caveat: approximate, for orientation, never authority.

Read more: Toolkit §4 — fetch_boundaries.py · Manual §8 — borders are orientation, not authority

Provenance or it doesn't compile
Every engine refuses unsourced content: a MapSpec without a provenance note, a journey stop without evidence + source, a network edge without type/evidence/citation — all are build errors, not warnings. The historian's hard line, made mechanical. The note you see in each map's panel is the artifact of that rule.

Read more: Toolkit §1 — architecture & principles · Manual §10 — 'provenance_note is required'

Layered controls — lenses, filters, drawer
Controls like a search engine: newcomers get five one-click lenses (Transmission · Lives · Texts · Themes · Density) that reconfigure everything at once; regulars get a filter bar (theme, colour-by, layer chips); power users get an advanced drawer (widths, sizes, opacity, tooltips). The bet: progressive disclosure beats both the empty-map-with-99-toggles and the locked-down infographic.

Read more: Manual §4 — reading a data map

The theme engine — four faces
One map, four complete visual identities, switchable live: Modern Atlas (clarity), Copperplate Engraving (the printed corpus), Illuminated Manuscript (courts and gold), Alchemical Noir (single lives, spectacle). Colour is assigned at runtime from the active theme's palette, so re-skinning never touches the data. The experiment: does aesthetic register change what a reader trusts and notices? Try the same map in Atlas and Noir.
